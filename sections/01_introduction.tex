\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Computer-use agents (CUAs) are emerging as one of the most consequential forms of agentic AI. Rather than stopping at language generation, these systems inspect interface state, interpret user goals, and carry out actions in websites, desktop software, and mobile applications. Recent progress in multimodal modeling, tool use, and interactive evaluation has pushed CUAs beyond narrow automation settings toward more general software operation. In practice, many of these systems already rely on hybrid control stacks that combine screen understanding with structural signals and GUI interaction with APIs, scripts, or command execution.

This shift changes not only what AI systems can do, but also how failure should be understood. In dialogue settings, a mistake often remains a textual error. In computer-use settings, the same mistake may become an unintended purchase, a deleted file, a permission change, an unsafe command, or a persistent workflow misconfiguration. The risk surface expands further as evaluation moves from reproducible web benchmarks such as Mind2Web and WebArena to open environments such as OSWorld, where agents must cope with unfamiliar interfaces, long-horizon tasks, visual ambiguity, and cross-application dependencies~\cite{deng2023mind2web,zhou2023webarena,xie2024osworld}. As a result, computer use is no longer only a benchmark problem; it is increasingly a systems, security, and governance problem.

A useful signal of this transition is \textbf{OpenClaw}. Public descriptions portray it not as a closed benchmark artifact, but as a local-first personal assistant connected to persistent communication channels such as WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, Discord, and iMessage~\cite{openclaw2026website,ibm2026openclaw}. Whether or not OpenClaw becomes a dominant platform is secondary. What it makes visible is a broader systems question: once a CUA is embedded in live channels, tools, memory, sessions, and permissions, capability can no longer be studied independently from authority, trust boundaries, and operational control.

Existing work addresses important aspects of this broader problem. Some studies focus on GUI-agent architecture, OS-agent environments, and multimodal agent ecosystems, while others examine safety benchmarks, misuse evaluation, prompt injection, and agent security frameworks~\cite{nguyen2025gui,hu2025agents,tang2025survey,kuntz2025harm,tur2025safearena,liu2025wainjectbench,adapala2025aegis,lazer2026survey}. However, these perspectives are still largely developed in parallel. As a result, the field lacks a systems-level account of how architectural choices, deployment decisions, runtime interaction, and post-deployment change jointly shape CUA capability, failure modes, and control requirements.

More specifically, prior GUI-agent and OS-agent studies mainly clarify \emph{architectural design choices}, benchmark construction, and platform coverage~\cite{nguyen2025gui,hu2025agents,tang2025survey}. Safety benchmarks such as OS-Harm, SafeArena, and WAInjectBench make \emph{action-level misuse and attack exposure} measurable, but do not by themselves explain how such failures arise from system design or deployment configuration~\cite{kuntz2025harm,tur2025safearena,liu2025wainjectbench}. Broader agent-security efforts, such as Aegis and recent agentic-AI security analyses, contribute important protocol, governance, and risk-analysis perspectives, yet they do not explicitly center the perception--decision--execution loop and deployment-specific characteristics that distinguish CUAs from more general agent systems~\cite{adapala2025aegis,lazer2026survey}. Table~\ref{tab:related_work_comparison} summarizes this positioning relative to representative prior work and benchmarks.

This paper studies general-purpose CUAs across GUI, OS, and web environments, treating them as end-to-end interactive systems rather than isolated benchmark models. We focus on how perception, reasoning, action interfaces, execution authority, and operating context interact in realistic deployments, while excluding dialogue-only agents, traditional rule-based robotic process automation, and embodied robotics except where they directly inform CUA design and governance.

To address the gap above, we analyze CUAs through a joint architecture--lifecycle perspective and make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Integrated architecture--lifecycle framework.} We organize the design space of computer-use agents using a unified \emph{Perception--Decision--Execution} architecture connected to a four-stage lifecycle of \emph{Creation, Deployment, Operation, and Maintenance}.
  \item \textbf{Security and risk analysis for CUAs.} We systematize the security landscape of computer-use agents through a lifecycle-aware threat model covering prompt injection, interface deception, tool and privilege misuse, memory compromise, and extension-layer or supply-chain failures.
  \item \textbf{Governance framing for open computer-use ecosystems.} We outline governance mechanisms for deploying CUAs in realistic computing environments, including evaluation and benchmarking, auditing and provenance, permission and sandboxing controls, and accountability across stakeholders.
\end{itemize}
