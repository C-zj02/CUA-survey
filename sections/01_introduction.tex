\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The recent rise of \emph{agentic AI} is transforming large language models from passive responders into systems that can \emph{perceive}, \emph{reason}, and \emph{act} in interactive environments. This transition is driven not only by advances in language modeling, but also by multimodal perception, tool-use interfaces, code execution, and increasingly realistic interactive benchmarks. A prominent manifestation of this trend is \textbf{Computer-Use Agents (CUAs)}: agents that observe and manipulate computer environments such as web pages, desktop operating systems, and mobile applications in order to accomplish user-specified goals. In practice, CUAs often operate through \emph{hybrid action interfaces} that combine GUI-level interactions (e.g., clicking, typing, scrolling) with programmatic operations such as API calls, scripting, or command execution.

Compared with dialogue-only assistants, CUAs substantially enlarge the real-world impact surface of AI systems. Their outputs are no longer confined to text generation, but may directly trigger side effects in external environments, including unintended purchases, data deletion, permission changes, unsafe tool invocation, or persistent system misconfiguration. Reflecting this shift, recent research has moved from structured and reproducible web benchmarks such as Mind2Web and WebArena to more open-ended computer-use settings such as OSWorld, where agents must cope with unfamiliar interfaces, non-stationary visual layouts, long-horizon tasks, and cross-application dependencies~\cite{deng2023mind2web,zhou2023webarena,xie2024osworld}. At the same time, these capabilities are beginning to appear in open deployment settings, suggesting that computer-use is becoming not only a benchmark problem, but also a systems, safety, and governance problem.

This transition exposes a central challenge for the field: although CUA capabilities are advancing rapidly, the research landscape remains fragmented across \emph{architectural design}, \emph{evaluation environments}, \emph{deployment practice}, and \emph{safety analysis}. Existing studies and surveys have provided valuable perspectives on GUI agents, OS agents, and multimodal agent ecosystems, while safety-oriented work has examined misuse, prompt injection, and harmful action benchmarks~\cite{nguyen2025gui,hu2025agents,tang2025survey,kuntz2025harm,tur2025safearena,liu2025wainjectbench}. However, these lines of work are often treated separately. As a result, the field still lacks a unified account of how CUAs are built, how architectural choices interact with real deployment constraints, where failures emerge over the lifecycle of a system, and what governance mechanisms are needed once such agents operate beyond controlled benchmarks.

In this survey, we focus on \textbf{general-purpose computer-use agents} that interact with GUI, OS, and web environments through multimodal perception and environment-grounded action. Our emphasis is not limited to benchmark performance; instead, we examine CUAs as end-to-end systems whose behavior emerges from the interaction among perception, reasoning, action interfaces, execution authority, and operational context. We do not aim to comprehensively cover dialogue-only LLM agents, traditional rule-based robotic process automation, or embodied robotic agents, except where they directly inform the design or governance of CUAs.

A useful motivating example for this transition is \textbf{OpenClaw}, which illustrates how CUA-like capabilities are moving toward persistent, user-facing deployment. Public descriptions portray OpenClaw not merely as a benchmarked GUI automation model, but as a personal AI assistant operating on user devices, connected to communication channels such as WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, Discord, and iMessage through an extensible and local-first control stack~\cite{openclaw2026website,ibm2026openclaw}. We use OpenClaw in this paper as a \emph{deployment exemplar} rather than a central object of evaluation: it helps highlight why always-on access, persistent memory, cross-channel interaction, and execution authority make computer-use agents qualitatively different from benchmark-only systems.

Motivated by this gap between benchmark-centered progress and deployment-facing complexity, we present a \textbf{lifecycle-oriented synthesis of the CUA ecosystem}. Our central claim is that CUAs are best understood not only through their task performance, but through the interaction between \emph{architectural choices}, \emph{system lifecycle}, and \emph{risk and governance surfaces}. Specifically, we make three contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Integrated architecture--lifecycle synthesis.} We organize the design space of computer-use agents using a unified \emph{Perception--Decision--Execution} architecture, and connect it to a four-stage lifecycle spanning \emph{Creation, Deployment, Execution, and Maintenance}. This synthesis clarifies how design choices shape capability, robustness, and failure modes across system stages.
  \item \textbf{Security and risk analysis for CUAs.} We systematize the security landscape of computer-use agents through a lifecycle-aware threat model covering prompt injection, interface deception, tool and privilege misuse, memory compromise, and extension-layer or supply-chain failures, and relate these risks to concrete triggering conditions and control points.
  \item \textbf{Governance framing for open computer-use ecosystems.} We outline governance mechanisms for the deployment of CUAs in realistic computing environments, including evaluation and benchmarking, auditing and provenance, permission and sandboxing controls, and accountability across stakeholders.
\end{itemize}

Taken together, this survey aims to provide an integrative map of the CUA field: not only \emph{how} computer-use agents are built, but also \emph{how} they are deployed, \emph{where} they fail, and \emph{what} technical and governance mechanisms are needed for trustworthy operation at scale.
