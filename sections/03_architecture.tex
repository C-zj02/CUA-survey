\section{Architectural Evolution of Computer-Use Agents: A Tri-Layer Framework}
\label{sec:architecture}

The rapid progress of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has transformed Computer-Use Agents (CUAs) from narrow automation pipelines into increasingly general-purpose systems capable of interacting with open-ended graphical user interfaces (GUIs). For survey purposes, however, the important question is not which system came first, but which architectural choice actually moves capability, failure behavior, and controllability. A more informative perspective is therefore to ask how an agent converts a raw screen observation into an intentional action, and which design decisions dominate that transformation. To this end, we organize the architectural evolution of CUAs into three tightly coupled layers: the \textit{Perception Layer}, the \textit{Decision Layer}, and the \textit{Execution Layer}. Rather than serving only as a descriptive categorization, this decomposition provides a comparative lens for understanding capability formation, failure propagation, and system-level trade-offs. Recent studies further suggest that this tri-layer view remains a useful organizing principle for CUA research, even as memory, reflection, evaluation, and safety increasingly emerge as cross-cutting concerns rather than fully separable architectural layers \cite{tang2025survey,shi2025trustworthygui,li2025summaryrlgui}.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:tri_layer_architecture}, the three layers form a closed control loop. The agent first grounds raw observations into an operational state representation, then transforms that state into task-conditioned plans, and finally instantiates those plans through executable actions whose consequences reshape the next observed state. Accordingly, the architectural question is not merely what modules a CUA contains, but how these modules jointly determine portability, robustness, controllability, and safety in real environments.

\subsection{Perception Layer: Structured Reconstruction of Screen States}

The Perception Layer addresses the first prerequisite of computer use: the agent must convert a human-readable visual interface into a machine-usable state representation. The core comparative result in this layer is straightforward: no current representation simultaneously maximizes portability, precision, and robustness. Since the central challenge is to recover usable interface state from heterogeneous and often incomplete observations, the main comparison is not among papers but among representation strategies. Current methods fall into three dominant paradigms: \emph{DOM/A11y-based parsing}, \emph{pure vision-based parsing}, and \emph{hybrid modality grounding}. Each paradigm answers the same underlying question differently: should GUI state be recovered from explicit structure, from pixels alone, or from a fusion of both?

\textbf{DOM/A11y-based Parsing.}  
The first paradigm relies on structured signals exposed by the environment, such as Document Object Model (DOM) trees or Accessibility (A11y) hierarchies. Representative systems such as Mind2Web \cite{deng2023mind2web} introduce candidate filtering mechanisms to compress long DOM trees into manageable contexts, while WebArena \cite{zhou2023webarena} heavily leverages structured HTML state to enable deterministic observation and evaluation. The strength of this paradigm lies in its precision: textual metadata, element attributes, and interaction targets can often be extracted reliably and with minimal ambiguity. Nevertheless, this precision comes at the cost of environmental dependence. Once an agent moves beyond browsers or accessible software stacks into rendering-heavy applications, remote desktops, or virtualized environments, such structured signals may become unavailable or brittle. Therefore, DOM/A11y parsing improves controllability, but its portability remains fundamentally constrained by platform support.

\textbf{Pure Vision-based Parsing.}  
To overcome this dependence on system APIs, a second line of work treats GUI understanding as a visual grounding problem over raw screenshots. OmniParser \cite{lu2024omniparser} removes DOM dependency and reconstructs GUI elements directly from screenshots using purely visual cues. CogAgent \cite{hong2024cogagent} adopts a dual-branch cross-attention architecture for high-resolution screen understanding, allowing the model to capture fine-grained icons and dense interface regions. Likewise, Fuyu-8B \cite{fuyu-8b} and Qwen-VL \cite{bai2023versatile} demonstrate that large vision-language models can localize interface elements and extract structured information from complex layouts with little or no environment-specific instrumentation. Compared with DOM-based systems, pure vision approaches are substantially more platform-agnostic and thus better aligned with the goal of general computer use. At the same time, however, they shift the burden toward robust grounding: OCR errors, icon ambiguity, small-target localization failures, and resolution sensitivity can all propagate into downstream planning mistakes. Thus, pure visual parsing gains generality, but it also makes system performance more tightly coupled to perception fidelity.

\textbf{Hybrid Modality Grounding.}  
Recent state-of-the-art systems increasingly adopt hybrid perception pipelines that combine visual evidence with structural priors. ScreenAI \cite{baechler2024screenai} integrates OCR and screen-layout understanding into a richer textual representation of UI structure. Ferret-UI \cite{you2024ferret} incorporates coordinate prediction into multimodal token streams to improve mobile GUI grounding. Similarly, SeeClick \cite{cheng2024seeclick}, TRISHUL \cite{singh2025trishul}, and complete-screen parsing approaches \cite{gurbuz2026moving} move beyond sparse point-level grounding toward denser and more hierarchical screen reconstruction. Importantly, these methods should not be viewed merely as compromises between structure and vision. Rather, they reflect a broader trend toward \emph{soft structuralization}: the agent recovers actionable topology from visual observations while retaining the flexibility to operate in open environments. Consequently, hybrid grounding is emerging not simply as an engineering patch, but as a more realistic representation strategy for heterogeneous GUI ecosystems.

Taken together, the Perception Layer is best understood through four core dimensions: \emph{portability}, \emph{grounding precision}, \emph{dependence on exposed structure}, and \emph{robustness to interface variation}. The non-obvious trade-off is that the representations that maximize deterministic control are usually the least portable, while the representations that maximize portability are usually the most vulnerable to ambiguity and deceptive cues. Recent work therefore points toward two converging directions: \emph{fine-grained executable grounding}, where perception must localize elements with enough precision for direct control \cite{yuan2025segui}, and \emph{anticipatory state modeling}, where perception begins to model likely future states rather than only current observations \cite{luo2025vimo}. In practical terms, perception quality should now be judged not only by recognition accuracy, but by whether it preserves enough trustworthy state for downstream planning and safe action.

\subsection{Decision Layer: Intent Reasoning and Long-Horizon Planning}

If the Perception Layer determines what the agent knows about the current interface, the Decision Layer determines what the agent intends to do next and why. Here the key comparative divide is not between ``simple'' and ``advanced'' planners, but between systems that merely propose plausible next steps and systems that preserve task coherence over time. As CUA benchmarks have moved toward open-ended and cross-application tasks, decision-making has evolved into a substantially more difficult problem involving state tracking, error recovery, and long-horizon decomposition. The challenge is no longer to generate the next plausible click, but to maintain task coherence over extended interaction trajectories.

Because the main bottleneck of modern GUI decision-making lies in stabilizing long-horizon reasoning under uncertainty, the most informative way to organize recent methods is by the mechanism used to preserve decision quality over time. From this perspective, existing approaches can be grouped into three broad strategies: \emph{memory-augmented planning}, \emph{closed-loop reflection and correction}, and \emph{hierarchical or multi-agent decomposition}. These categories are not strictly exclusive, but they capture the dominant architectural responses to the same core problem: how to sustain coherent intent over long and error-prone interaction sequences.

\textbf{Memory-Augmented Planning.}  
One major response to this challenge is to strengthen the decision process with explicit memory mechanisms. AppAgent \cite{zhang2025appagent} separates exploration from execution, allowing the agent to accumulate a reusable memory bank of prior UI interactions for retrieval during later tasks. InfiGUIAgent \cite{liu2025infiguiagent} similarly combines reasoning and reflection with trajectory recall to avoid repetitive loops and unstable action sequences. More generally, baselines on OSWorld \cite{xie2024osworld} reveal that prolonged computer interaction quickly becomes unreliable without some form of context-sensitive retrieval or trajectory compression. These studies collectively suggest that long-horizon GUI planning depends less on one-shot instruction following than on the agent's ability to preserve continuity across changing screens. In this sense, memory is not merely an auxiliary feature; it is increasingly a prerequisite for stable decision-making in realistic environments.

\textbf{Closed-loop Reflection and Correction.}  
A second trend is the integration of explicit feedback and correction loops. Because GUI tasks often consist of many irreversible or weakly reversible steps, small early errors can cascade into major task failure. WebVoyager \cite{he2024webvoyager} introduces visual-textual trajectory reasoning that enables backtracking when a previous action is judged incorrect. ``You Only Look at Screens'' \cite{zhang2024you} proposes a multimodal Chain-of-Action formulation that requires the model to articulate intermediate reasoning before emitting concrete actions, thereby improving both interpretability and self-correction. ReInAgent \cite{jia2025reinagent} further extends this line with human-in-the-loop active questioning under uncertainty, while Mobile-Agent \cite{wang2024mobile} relies on frequent re-planning conditioned on fresh visual feedback. What unifies these approaches is the recognition that robust decision-making in CUAs cannot remain purely feedforward. Instead, the agent must continuously test whether its internal plan still aligns with the external environment and revise that plan when divergence is detected.

\textbf{Hierarchical and Multi-Agent Decomposition.}  
As task scope expands across applications and interfaces, a monolithic policy often becomes difficult to scale. This has motivated hierarchical and multi-agent decision designs that divide cognitive labor across abstraction levels. UFO \cite{zhang2025ufo} adopts a dual-agent formulation with an Application Agent for high-level software routing and an Action Agent for local operations. CoAct-1 \cite{song2025coact} introduces a central orchestrator that decomposes cross-application workflows into coordinated subroutines. Such approaches are important not because more agents are inherently better, but because decomposition limits the scope of each decision problem. High-level reasoning can focus on workflow structure, while low-level agents specialize in interface-constrained execution. Therefore, hierarchical design reduces context overload and improves modularity, especially in long workflows that would otherwise overwhelm a single reasoning loop.

The Decision Layer is best compared along dimensions such as \emph{long-horizon coherence}, \emph{error recovery capability}, \emph{memory cost}, and \emph{decomposition efficiency}. The key analytical point is that decision quality now depends less on generating a clever next step and more on preventing local uncertainty from turning into global task drift \cite{wu2025guireflection,li2025mobileuse}. Because this layer converts ambiguous observations into operational commitment, long-horizon safety and constraint retention are primarily decision problems rather than only alignment problems.

\subsection{Execution Layer: Instantiated Mapping of Action Spaces}

Once an agent has formed an intention, that intention must be realized through an executable interface to the environment. The decisive comparison in this layer concerns action abstraction: what matters most is not the target platform alone, but how much semantic distance separates model output from real authority. Although execution is sometimes treated as a straightforward downstream component, its design strongly influences not only efficiency but also auditability, recoverability, and security exposure. In practice, the choice of action space determines what kinds of plans are feasible, how errors manifest, and how easily system behavior can be constrained.

Because the core design question of this layer is how agent intent is bound to a controllable action interface, execution strategies are most usefully distinguished by the level of abstraction at which the environment exposes executable operations. Recent systems can be grouped into three broad paradigms: \emph{atomic mouse/keyboard emulation}, \emph{code-as-action or API invocation}, and \emph{macro-action or continuous-control formulations}. This abstraction-oriented categorization is more useful than grouping systems by platform alone, because it directly reveals the trade-offs among universality, efficiency, auditability, and control.

\textbf{Atomic Mouse/Keyboard Emulation.}  
The most general execution paradigm uses only low-level coordinates, gestures, and keystrokes. Cradle \cite{tan2024cradle} demonstrates that even highly complex environments such as 3D games can be controlled through purely atomic inputs. AndroidEnv \cite{toyama2021androidenv} similarly formalizes mobile interaction as touch, swipe, and keyboard primitives, a formulation later adopted by visual agents such as UI-TARS-2 and GUI-Bee \cite{wang2025ui,fan2025gui}. The major advantage of this approach is universality: if an interface is visible, atomic execution can in principle operate on it regardless of hidden APIs or platform conventions. However, this universality comes with a substantial cost. Low-level action spaces are high-entropy, error-sensitive, and tightly coupled to precise grounding. A minor localization mistake may immediately cause a misclick, unintended drag, or cascading deviation from the intended task. From a security perspective, such interfaces also broaden the surface for clickjacking, action rebinding, and environmental manipulation \cite{qian2026zero,zhang2026realistic}. Hence, atomic control maximizes flexibility, but it also amplifies both execution fragility and safety risk.

\textbf{Code-as-Action and API Invocation.}  
At the opposite end of the spectrum, some systems replace or augment GUI interaction with direct programmatic execution. OSCopilot \cite{wu2024copilot} uses generated Python and Bash code to manipulate operating systems and demonstrates efficiency gains in system tasks such as file management. GUI-360 \cite{mu2025gui} advocates a hybrid execution space in which agents can invoke application-native macros (e.g., Excel VBA) alongside direct UI manipulation. WebArena \cite{zhou2023webarena}, although narrower in scope, also standardizes browser-side JavaScript events to maintain deterministic execution. These systems illustrate a broader architectural point: for many real software tasks, simulating human clicks is not the most efficient or reliable way to act. Higher-level execution interfaces compress many low-level actions into a smaller number of semantically meaningful operations. Yet this compression also raises the stakes of each action. Least privilege, provenance tracking, and sandbox isolation become more critical because a single API or code call may encapsulate multiple consequential side effects \cite{jones2025systematization,zhang2025mcp}. Thus, high-level execution improves efficiency and reproducibility, but it requires much stronger control over capability boundaries.

\textbf{Macro-Action and Continuous Control.}  
Between these two extremes lies a growing class of macro-action and continuous-control formulations. AppAgentX \cite{jiang2025appagentx} introduces mechanisms for autonomously encapsulating frequent action sequences into reusable higher-level routines. ScreenAgent \cite{niu2024screenagent} and OmegaUse \cite{zhang2026omegause} further model parameterized continuous control for operations such as scrolling and drag-and-drop, allowing one command to specify a cohesive action trajectory. These approaches are attractive because they reduce decision burden and shrink the search space of low-level action composition. At the same time, however, abstraction creates new verification challenges: when one macro encapsulates multiple impactful substeps, it becomes harder to attribute failure, interrupt unsafe execution, or audit system behavior step by step \cite{zhang2024agent,miculicich2025veriguard}. Therefore, macro-abstraction can improve efficiency and stability, but only at the cost of more complex validation and oversight.

Overall, the Execution Layer should be evaluated along dimensions including \emph{action granularity}, \emph{execution efficiency}, \emph{auditability}, \emph{privilege requirements}, and \emph{recoverability from failure}. The core trade-off is straightforward but consequential: interfaces that maximize universality also maximize exposure to low-level execution error, while interfaces that maximize efficiency also enlarge the blast radius of each action. Recent systems further show that execution is expanding from a GUI-only action space toward \emph{hybrid execution}, where code and GUI control are coordinated within the same loop \cite{song2025coact}. This reinforces the view that execution is not merely downstream actuation; it is the architectural boundary at which intent acquires real authority.

\subsection{Cross-layer Coupling and Architectural Implications}

The foregoing analysis shows that the three layers should not be read as independent modules arranged in sequence, but as tightly coupled components of a closed-loop interactive system. Architectural progress rarely comes from one layer in isolation: perception errors reshape the state seen by the Decision Layer, decision complexity determines the abstraction demanded from the Execution Layer, and execution outcomes redefine the next perceptual input.

This cross-layer view also clarifies why architectural trade-offs reappear as reliability and safety issues. A perception pipeline that favors portability over structure can increase grounding ambiguity, a decision policy optimized for autonomy can magnify accumulated planning error, and an execution interface that compresses actions for efficiency can enlarge the impact radius of a mistaken command. In this sense, architecture is already governance in latent form.

Recent survey literature consistently retains perception, planning/decision, and interaction/acting as the architectural backbone of GUI agents, while treating memory, exploration, evaluation, and safety as capabilities that cut across the pipeline rather than forming fully independent layers \cite{tang2025survey,li2025summaryrlgui}. This interpretation is also supported by benchmark advances. For example, macOSWorld shows that multilingual transfer and deception attacks stress all three layers simultaneously: perception must remain robust across languages and misleading screens, decision-making must detect inconsistency and recover from deceptive states, and execution must avoid unsafe or irreversible actions under uncertainty \cite{yang2025macosworld}. Likewise, the growing trustworthiness literature argues that security, reliability, transparency, and evaluation should not be treated as post hoc add-ons, but as constraints that shape architectural choices from the beginning \cite{shi2025trustworthygui}.

The tri-layer framework is therefore best understood as a \emph{backbone} rather than an exhaustive decomposition: memory and reflection primarily reinforce the Decision Layer, fine-grained grounding and world modeling refine the Perception Layer, and hybrid code-plus-GUI control expands the Execution Layer, while evaluation and safety remain cross-layer concerns. At the same time, this decomposition remains fundamentally static. It explains where capability resides and where failure first surfaces inside the agent, but less well how those capabilities are produced, connected to real platforms, stressed in open environments, and revised after deployment.

A fuller account of CUA reliability therefore requires moving beyond architectural composition alone. Once the tri-layer backbone is established, the next question is how that backbone is instantiated and governed over time: how perceptual grounding is created, how action interfaces are deployed, how decision-making is stressed during live operation, and how the system is maintained as interfaces, workflows, and threat models evolve. The next section accordingly shifts from architectural decomposition to lifecycle analysis, introducing a four-stage view---Creation, Deployment, Operation, and Maintenance---that complements the tri-layer framework by explaining how CUA capabilities are formed, exposed, and sustained in practice.
