\section{Lifecycle}
\label{sec:lifecycle}

\subsection{Background and Problem Statement}
Computer-Use Agents (CUA) perceive and operate graphical user interfaces (GUIs) on desktops, web pages, or mobile devices via actions such as clicking, typing, dragging, and tool invocation, in order to accomplish long-horizon tasks. Compared with conventional scripted automation, CUAs are driven by foundation models and therefore generalize better across interfaces and tasks. However, they also introduce distinct security and safety risks: (i) the interaction target is an open, dynamic, and potentially adversarial software environment; (ii) the action space includes high-impact operations (e.g., sending messages, payments, deletions, installations, executing commands); and (iii) the input chain can be influenced by external content (e.g., indirect prompt injection through webpages or documents).

Existing studies have surveyed the broader CUA landscape and challenges~[5], and have further summarized safety and security threats with structured taxonomies~[3]. In addition, systematizations of CUA vulnerabilities suggest that many risks stem from missing \emph{system-level mechanisms} (e.g., weak provenance, fragile interaction binding, unclear privilege boundaries) rather than purely model-level misalignment~[4]. Motivated by a security-engineering view akin to ``create--deploy--operate--maintain,'' we organize the CUA lifecycle into four phases and summarize key activities, representative technical directions, and evaluation practices for each phase.

\subsection{Lifecycle Phases and Overview}
We divide the CUA lifecycle into four phases:
(1) \textbf{Creation/Development}: methods and prototypes are designed and trained, emphasizing capability modeling, safety baselines, and evaluability;
(2) \textbf{Deployment}: prototypes are integrated into real environments, emphasizing privilege boundaries, isolation, auditability, and controllable system interfaces;
(3) \textbf{Operation}: online execution, emphasizing runtime monitoring, detection, correction, and human confirmation loops;
(4) \textbf{Maintenance}: long-term operation and iteration, emphasizing continuous evaluation, vulnerability response, data/policy updates, and governance.

\subsection{Creation/Development}
\label{sec:creation}
The goal of the creation phase is to ``design in'' safety early, making safety an object that is \emph{specifiable, learnable, measurable, and integrable} rather than something patched post-deployment. We highlight four key activities.

\subsubsection{Threat Modeling and Risk Taxonomy}
CUA risk does not only arise from model outputs; it emerges from the closed loop of \emph{perceive--reason--act}. The perception layer can be manipulated via UI spoofing and deceptive affordances; the reasoning layer can be hijacked by prompt injection, goal redirection, or distribution shift; and the action layer can execute over-privileged or high-impact operations with irreversible side effects. Prior work provides structured taxonomies of safety and security threats for computer-using agents, distinguishing internal failure modes from external attacks and summarizing defense directions~[3]. A systematization of vulnerabilities further emphasizes CUA-specific system risks such as missing provenance (enabling indirect prompt injection), weak interaction binding (enabling clickjacking), and unclear privilege boundaries~[4]. Together, these efforts provide the ``design inputs'' for creation-stage security: threat identification, attack-surface analysis, and safety requirement extraction.

\subsubsection{Safety Benchmarks and Metrics}
Without shared evaluation, safety improvements cannot be reproduced or compared. Recent benchmarks refine evaluation along multiple dimensions:
(i) \textbf{long-horizon safety awareness}, where LPS-BENCH measures whether agents consistently obey safety constraints across extended plans and multi-step trajectories~[32];
(ii) \textbf{harmfulness measurement}, which quantifies misuse potential and highlights that agents may exhibit ``blind'' goal pursuit that ignores harmful side effects~[30,27];
and (iii) \textbf{system-oriented safety benchmarks} such as OS-HARM, which evaluates safety failures in realistic settings and provides a common testbed for guardrails and alignment methods~[38].
These benchmarks convert safety from qualitative claims into quantitative signals, enabling fair comparison among training strategies, guardrail mechanisms, and system designs.

\subsubsection{Training and Alignment for Safe Control}
Creation-stage alignment must balance task success with safety-constraint satisfaction. A line of work studies policy optimization for GUI agents, including shifting from offline to on-policy training to improve generalization and behavioral stability~[22]. Other efforts use evolutionary/self-improving training paradigms that adapt policies through task interaction~[17]. Scalable synthetic experience is another route: EvoCUA emphasizes learning from synthetic trajectories to evolve CUAs across broader task distributions~[21]. In practice, these techniques provide the methodological basis for learning safety-aware behavior, but they also raise a key question: which safety guarantees should be enforced by learning alone, and which require system-level mechanisms at deployment and runtime (see Table~\ref{tab:lifecycle_overview} and the next subsections).

\subsubsection{Safe Interfaces and Action Abstractions}
Many CUA risks stem from low-level, high-entropy action spaces. A promising engineering direction is to reduce and constrain actions via interface/primitive design, separating \emph{policy} (semantic planning) from \emph{mechanism} (controlled execution). Declarative, LLM-friendly interfaces advocate controlled primitives and structured interaction, reducing instability and attackability associated with pixel-level control~[2]. Related interface thinking appears in agent-computer interface work for software engineering, where careful abstractions improve reliability and controllability~[51]. From a security perspective, constrained primitives can force high-impact actions into auditable, authorizable operations, providing a natural ``hook'' for deployment-phase access control.

\subsection{Deployment}
\label{sec:deployment}
Deployment translates creation-stage methods into real-world systems by enforcing \emph{system boundaries}: privilege, isolation, provenance, pre-release gating, and red teaming. Compared with creation (algorithm/model-centric), deployment is system-security and governance-centric.

\subsubsection{System-Level Risks and Missing Mechanisms}
Systematization studies indicate that many real-world CUA risks originate from missing system mechanisms rather than solely from model misalignment: lack of provenance prevents agents from distinguishing user intent from externally injected instructions; weak interaction binding enables UI spoofing and click hijacking; and unclear privilege boundaries allow overreach and unguarded high-impact actions~[4]. Hence, deployment should prioritize these system capabilities instead of relying only on prompt rules.

\subsubsection{Least Privilege and Access Control}
In production, CUAs often access files, browsers, enterprise apps, or third-party services, requiring configurable authorization policies. Work on access control frameworks for CUAs emphasizes achieving security while maintaining efficiency and usability~[49]. From a security-engineering standpoint, such mechanisms should support:
(1) \emph{action-level permissions} (what operations are allowed),
(2) \emph{object-level permissions} (which accounts/files/domains/apps are accessible),
(3) \emph{contextual conditions} (task, risk level, and whether human approval is required),
and (4) \emph{auditability} (policy hits and execution traces are traceable).

\subsubsection{System-Level Defense and Pre-release Red Teaming}
Beyond static authorization, deployment must consider system-level defenses against adversarial environments. CAMELS frames system-level security risks and defenses in the full interaction pipeline~[14]. Realistic adversarial testing such as RedTeamCUA provides a red-teaming process and adversarial cases to stress-test agents before release~[43]. In practice, deployment-stage red teaming should be operationalized into: (i) pre-release blocking rules/policies, (ii) regression test suites for maintenance, and (iii) runtime monitoring and alert rules for operation.

\subsection{Operation}
\label{sec:operation}
Operation aims to turn safety into a \emph{closed-loop control system}: monitor $\rightarrow$ detect $\rightarrow$ correct/block $\rightarrow$ rollback/confirm. Because real environments are open and potentially adversarial, runtime defenses often determine the practical safety floor.

\subsubsection{Runtime Defense and Real-time Blocking}
AgentSentinel proposes an end-to-end, real-time security defense framework that continuously detects and intercepts risks during execution~[9]. Compared with input-only filtering, runtime frameworks focus on the \emph{action trajectory}: they trigger checks before critical steps (e.g., sending, paying, deleting), log auditable evidence after actions, and apply rollback/termination when anomalies are detected. This naturally aligns with ``defense-in-depth'' principles.

\subsubsection{Predictive Guardrails and Reasoning Correction}
Predictive guardrails attempt to anticipate risky trajectories before damage occurs. SAFEPRED introduces a predictive guardrail perspective to reduce the cost of post-hoc recovery~[44]. MirrorGuard focuses on simulation-to-real reasoning correction, adjusting agent reasoning when unsafe or erroneous decisions arise~[29]. Together, these works highlight an operational core challenge: \emph{how to detect high-risk trajectories early and correct them without substantially reducing task success or efficiency}.

\subsubsection{Off-task/Misaligned Action Detection and Human-in-the-loop (HITL)}
Operational failures often appear as ``apparently successful but actually off-task'' behaviors, or ``goal achievement at the expense of safety.'' Work on detecting and correcting misaligned/off-task actions provides mechanisms to pull agents back when they drift or violate constraints~[60]. Another complementary direction is completion verification and feedback: vision-based judges can assess whether tasks are truly completed and provide corrective feedback to reduce repeated or erroneous actions~[1]. In high-impact actions, HITL confirmation remains essential in practice: systems must be able to escalate risks into confirmable events and present interpretable evidence (logs, screenshots, intent summaries) for user approval.

\subsection{Maintenance}
\label{sec:maintenance}
Maintenance emphasizes long-term operation and continual improvement. Safety is not a one-time deliverable; it co-evolves with environment changes, model updates, and adversarial tactics.

\subsubsection{Continuous Evaluation and Regression}
Model/interface updates can regress safety properties, motivating continuous benchmarks and regression testing. Benchmarks such as OS-HARM can serve as periodic regression suites~[38], while harmfulness measurement helps track misuse risk trends over time~[30]. The maintenance objective is to operationalize evaluation into scheduled scoring, release gating thresholds, failure attribution, and patch prioritization.

\subsubsection{Vulnerability Management and Incident Response}
CUAs may be exploited to perform vulnerability exploitation and extended attack chains. HackWorld provides an evaluation environment for web application vulnerability exploitation, enabling systematic measurement of offensive capability and potential risk~[25]. From a defensive perspective, such environments can also support ``attack regression'': turning known attack chains into persistent detection cases and verifying mitigations after patches.

\subsubsection{Data/Policy Evolution and Enterprise Governance}
A key maintenance thread is continual learning and policy updates. EvoCUA advocates evolving CUAs through scalable synthetic experience, aligning with a practical loop that converts online failures and adversarial cases into training data and policy updates~[21]. Enterprise deployment further demands stability, controllability, audit, and governance; enterprise-ready generalist agent research discusses system capabilities needed for operational readiness~[55].

\subsection{Discussion and Open Problems}
From a lifecycle perspective, several cross-phase open problems emerge:
(1) \textbf{Boundary between mechanism and learning:} which risks should be strictly enforced by mechanisms (privilege, isolation, controlled primitives) versus reduced by learning-based alignment? Interface abstractions~[2,51] and access control~[49] provide mechanism hooks, but their coupling with training-based alignment~[22,21] remains under-explored.
(2) \textbf{From benchmarks to operational gating:} how do benchmarks (e.g.,~[32,38,30]) translate into release gates, alert thresholds, and maintenance regressions? How should red-teaming suites~[43] connect with runtime defense~[9]?
(3) \textbf{Safety awareness in long-horizon tasks:} safety constraints may be forgotten or dominated by goals;~[32,27] observe this phenomenon, but maintaining constraints at runtime without excessive success-rate loss remains challenging.
(4) \textbf{Explainable auditing and accountability:} vulnerability systematizations emphasize provenance and interaction binding~[4], yet standardized, usable audit evidence chains are still limited.
