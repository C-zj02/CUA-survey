\section{Open Problems and Future Directions}
\label{sec:limitations}

The framework developed in this paper also clarifies what remains unresolved. Even with better benchmarks, stronger guardrails, and improved deployment practice, current CUA security still faces several open problems that limit reliable deployment at scale.

\subsection{Attribution and Task-Boundary Observability}
The distinction among scope overreach, objective corruption, and environmental misbinding is analytically useful, and this paper introduces a practical decision rule for applying it, but the framework remains difficult to operationalize in full generality. Off-task exploration and attacker-driven exfiltration can produce similar surface behavior, and what counts as ``on-task'' often remains underspecified in real workflows. Better telemetry, provenance, and causal tracing are therefore needed if attribution is to support actionable defense design rather than post hoc interpretation~\cite{jones2025systematization,foerster2026camels}.

\subsection{Benchmark Realism and Release Gating}
Current benchmarks still only partially capture real deployment conditions. Long-horizon workflows, cross-application dependencies, maintenance-stage drift, and multi-agent propagation remain underrepresented in common evaluation suites. As a result, benchmark success does not yet translate reliably into release readiness. A major open problem is to connect benchmark signals more tightly to deployment gating, regression testing, and post-release monitoring~\cite{kuntz2025harm,tian2025measuring,chen2026lps,zheng2026riskybench,wang2026pasb}.

\subsection{Cross-modal Injection and Perception Robustness}
Prompt injection in CUAs is not only a text problem. Visual anchors, imperceptible perturbations, deceptive layouts, and observation-to-action mismatches show that the attack surface spans both content and interface structure~\cite{liu2025wainjectbench,zhang2026realistic,qian2026zero}. Robust multimodal defenses remain immature, particularly when attacks exploit the coupling between perception and execution rather than reasoning alone.

\subsection{Capability--Safety Tension Under Tool Access}
Several recent studies suggest that stronger models may also be more dangerous once they are given richer tools, broader permissions, or stronger incentives to complete a task under pressure~\cite{tian2025measuring,sehwag2025propensitybench}. This raises a hard systems question: how should capability gains be translated into deployment policy? Future work should evaluate safety as a function of tool access, task difficulty, and operational pressure rather than as a static model property.

\subsection{Governance for Multi-agent and Tool Ecosystems}
CUAs increasingly operate inside tool registries, MCP-style ecosystems, and agent-to-agent platforms where capabilities can propagate socially as well as technically. In these environments, identity, attestation, registry hygiene, and content governance become core security requirements rather than administrative details~\cite{lee2024prompt,zhang2025mcp,wang2026asset,liu2026clawkeeper}. The field still lacks standardized mechanisms for governing capability diffusion across such open ecosystems.

Taken together, these open problems reinforce the main argument of the paper: CUA security cannot be reduced to better prompting or stronger model alignment alone. Progress requires jointly improving architectural robustness, deployment discipline, runtime control, and maintenance-stage governance.
