\begin{abstract}
Computer-use agents, which perceive on-screen content, reason over user goals, and execute GUI actions in desktop and web environments, are advancing rapidly. However, research on these agents remains fragmented across capability development, evaluation, safety, and governance. This paper systematizes the field through a unified three-layer architecture---Perception, Decision, and Execution---that captures key design choices in state representation, reasoning, and action grounding. We complement this view with a lifecycle perspective spanning creation, deployment, execution, and maintenance, showing how risks, responsibilities, and failure modes evolve from development to real-world operation. Building on these foundations, we develop a security threat model and attribution framework tailored to computer-use agents. This framework organizes emerging risks, including prompt injection, interface deception, tool and privilege misuse, and supply-chain compromise, and links them to concrete triggering conditions and defensive control points across the lifecycle. Finally, we outline a governance agenda covering evaluation and benchmarking, auditing and provenance, permission control and sandboxing, and cross-stakeholder accountability. Our goal is to provide a unified foundation for understanding how computer-use agents are built, where they fail, and how they can be made more verifiable, controllable, and sustainable at scale.
\end{abstract}
