\begin{abstract}
  As computer-use agents that perceive on-screen content, reason over goals, and execute GUI actions in real desktop and web environments advance rapidly, research in this space has become fragmented across capability building, evaluation, and safety governance. This paper systematizes the field through a unified three-layer architecture—Perception, Decision, and Execution—that captures key design variations in state representation, reasoning mechanisms, and action grounding. We complement this with a lifecycle perspective spanning creation, deployment, execution, and maintenance, highlighting how risks, responsibilities, and failure modes shift as agents move from development to real-world operation. Building on these foundations, we develop a security threat model and attribution lens tailored to computer-use agents, organizing emerging risks such as prompt injection, interface deception, tool and privilege misuse, and supply-chain compromise, and relating them to concrete triggering conditions and defensive levers across the lifecycle. Finally, we outline a forward-looking governance agenda covering evaluation and benchmarks, auditing and provenance, permissioning and sandboxing, and cross-stakeholder accountability—aiming to guide the development of computer-use agents that are verifiable, controllable, and sustainable at scale.
\end{abstract}
